Security deposits are one of the most common sources of landlord–tenant disputes in Florida — and almost all of them are avoidable. The rules are specific, and following them protects you. Here's a plain-English overview.

## Holding the deposit

Florida law (Chapter 83, Part II of the Florida Statutes) gives landlords a few options for holding a tenant's security deposit, including a separate non-interest-bearing account, an interest-bearing account, or posting a surety bond. Whichever you choose, the deposit generally must be kept separate from your own funds, and you must disclose to the tenant where and how it's held.

## The move-out clock

When a tenant moves out, timing is everything:

- If you do **not** intend to make any claim on the deposit, you generally must return it within **15 days**.
- If you **do** intend to keep some or all of it, you generally must send the tenant written notice by certified mail within **30 days**, stating your intention to impose a claim and the reason.
- The tenant then has a window to object before you can deduct.

Miss these deadlines and you can forfeit your right to keep any of the deposit — so a reliable process matters.

## What you can (and can't) deduct

You can generally deduct for unpaid rent and for damage beyond ordinary wear and tear. You **cannot** deduct for normal wear — faded paint, lightly worn carpet, small nail holes. The distinction between "damage" and "wear and tear" is where most disputes live, which is why thorough, dated move-in and move-out inspections with photos are a landlord's best protection.

## How professional management helps

A good property manager runs this entire process by the book: documented inspections, proper notices sent on time by the right method, and clean accounting. That documentation is what wins disputes — and more often, prevents them entirely.
