How much does property management cost in Tampa?

Everything you should know about fees in 2026 — and how to tell whether you're actually getting your money's worth.

If you own a rental in Tampa Bay, the first question on your mind is usually simple: what will a property manager actually cost me? The honest answer is that it depends on your property — but the ranges are predictable, and once you understand what drives them, it's easy to spot a fair deal.

The core management fee

Most full-service property managers in the Tampa area charge a monthly management fee between 7.5% and 10% of the rent they collect. The exact number depends on the complexity of the job: a single, newer home in a stable neighborhood sits at the low end, while older properties, multiple units, or homes that need more hands-on attention land higher.

At Tampa Bay Property Experts, our fee starts at 7.5% and scales with the work involved — and crucially, we only earn it when rent is actually collected. That keeps our incentives aligned with yours: keep good tenants in place and rent flowing.

Other fees to watch for

Beyond the monthly percentage, here are the line items that vary most between companies:

  • Leasing / tenant-placement fee — a one-time charge (often a half to one month's rent) to market the home, screen applicants, and sign a new lease.
  • Lease renewal fee — a smaller fee when a tenant renews instead of moving out.
  • Maintenance markups — some companies add a percentage on top of repair invoices. Ask directly.
  • Vacancy or setup fees — one-time onboarding charges. Many quality managers waive these.

The headline percentage matters, but the total cost picture is what counts. A "cheap" 6% manager who marks up every repair and nickel-and-dimes renewals can easily cost more than a transparent 8% manager who doesn't.

What you're actually paying for

Good management pays for itself in fewer vacancy days, better tenants, and protected property value. A strong manager handles marketing and listing, thorough screening, lease execution, rent collection, maintenance coordination, inspections, accounting, and legal compliance — the parts that quietly cost owners the most when they go wrong.

The single biggest hidden cost in self-management is a bad tenant. One eviction or one month of unexpected vacancy can wipe out a year of "savings" from skipping a manager.

How to know you're getting value

Ask any manager three questions: How do you screen tenants? What's your average days-on-market for a vacancy? And what's the all-in cost including every fee? Clear, confident answers are a good sign. Vague ones are not.
